Background

I began exploration of college basketball statistics around five years ago when I was copying tables from Basketball-Reference into Excel. I was using macros to reformat the data and designing my own algorithms to analyze a team's strength. My first algorithm correctly predicted the result of 29 of the 32 first round games in 2016's March Madness tournament and I was immediately hooked, not only on the basketball statistics but on the analysis itself.

Since then, my knowledge of data analysis tools and principles has expanded extensively and, with that, my methods have changed significantly. Currently, I am using Pandas and Jupyter Notebook to clean and organize the data before using Python to run the data through algorithms. Finally, I am using Matplotlib to visualize the results with a primary focus on the correlations between my algorithms and a team's win percentage.

Algorithm

While I examine the correlation of many variables with win percentage, my primary interest in the exploration of these statistics is the development of my own algorithm. Seventy-seven percent of the variation in win percentage for this season is explained through this algorithm, placing it at the same level as more established metrics.

Considerations

Due to Covid-19, many teams elected to either not play a basketball season, or to play an abbreviated schedule. This leads to larger than average variation in the number of games played and was the key factor in my decision to use win percentage rather than wins for correlations. A total of nine teams were dropped completely from the dataset because they did not play in any games this season.

As of now, all data is sourced through csv files from Basketball-Reference according to their data usage policies. Moving forward, I will be moving to web scraped data and developing more advanced algorithms that stem from my own accumulation of the data throughout the season, rather than looking at season totals.
